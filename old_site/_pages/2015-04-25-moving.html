I am pleased to announce that this summer, my laboratory will be moving to the University of Pittsburgh School of Medicine, in the Department of Microbiology and Molecular Genetics. I will likely be jointly appointed in the Department of Computational and Systems Biology and anticipate great interactions with researchers in the Biology Department of the University of Pittsburgh and in the Cystic Fibrosis Research Center.

The primary focus of the Cooper laboratory is to use genomics and experimental models to propel a virtuous cycle of inquiry using i) evolutionary-genetic and ecological theory, ii) laboratory models of evolution, and iii) pathogen evolution during human infections. We are especially interested in adaptation in the structured environments of bacterial biofilms, which cause a large fraction of human infections and serve as tractable models of multispecies communities and the ecosystem of many cancers.

WE ARE RECRUITING. Applications from creative postdoctoral scholars who share these goals and want to help us lead initiatives in evolutionary biology, genomics, and microbiome research at a Top-5 U.S. medical school are welcome. This is an exceptional opportunity to a launch a career in the in-demand field of evolutionary genomics.

I also welcome talented Ph.D. applicants, particularly those who are motivated to pursue independent fellowships. Graduate programs in which I hope to participate include the Interdisciplinary Biomedical Training Program, Computational Biology (joint with Carnegie Mellon), and Integrative Systems Biology.

Our laboratory will be supported by a generous startup, which will feature in-house Illumina sequencing and bioinformatic analysis, and a NIH-NIGMS R01 on mechanisms of biofilm adaptation, a NASA Astrobiology Institute award, and two collaborative awards from NSF.

More about our ongoing research, including why genome regions evolve at different rates, and an evolution-in-action curriculum for our nation’s high schools, are described on our webpages.
