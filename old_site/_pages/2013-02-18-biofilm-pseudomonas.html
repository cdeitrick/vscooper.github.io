The evolution and maintenance of diversity in Pseudomonas aeruginosa PA14 biofilms.

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is an opportunistic bacterial pathogen shown to have the capacity to cause chronic infections in the lungs of persons with cystic fibrosis (CF). These Pseudomonas lung infections are particularly difficult to treat due to their unpredictable patterns of evolutionary diversification.

Major Research Questions:

How can we deconvolute the evolutionary and ecological dynamics of chronic Pseudomonas infections?

What genetic or ecological changes define significant shifts that affect the function of the diverse population and its pathogenicity to the host

Our approach.

We use a biofilm experimental evolution method that allows us to study evolution in real time, as described previously.

The unpredictability of Pseudomonas ecology.

From K. Flynn: What you see above are representative samples of the phenotypic differences seen through time in replicate populations. These differences in colony development are caused by numerous genetic changes to the genome of this organism. By grouping isolates based on genetic history and phenotypic traits, we are able to identify what traits may underlie persistence as biofilm specialists. This increase in standing genetic variation in these populations likely contributes to the unpredictable fluctuations seen above and during chronic Pseudomonas infections.
